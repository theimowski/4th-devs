# How We Cleaned Up a Messy Docs Hub

When our documentation library crossed one hundred articles, it became obvious that the real problem was not volume. Readers could land on one useful page and still have no clear path to the next step.

We started with a [content audit](/guides/content-audit) to understand which articles were outdated, overlapping, or impossible to discover from the main navigation. That gave us a short list of pages that deserved a rewrite instead of another quick patch.

From there, every new article had to begin with an [editorial brief](/templates/editorial-brief). Without that step, writers kept mixing audience, search intent, and product context in the same draft, which made later editing much harder.

We also had to tighten our [tone of voice](/brand/tone-of-voice). Some pages sounded like release notes, some sounded like sales copy, and some were written like support tickets. The inconsistency made the whole knowledge base feel stitched together instead of designed.

The next fix was internal linking. We found plenty of articles that mentioned setup, onboarding, or taxonomy decisions, but almost none of them pointed readers toward the deeper explainer they actually needed.

After that, we revisited the overall [knowledge base structure](/knowledge-base/structure) so category pages, tutorials, and reference docs supported each other instead of competing for attention.

We also pulled our [customer onboarding checklist](/guides/onboarding-checklist) into the same ecosystem so new users could move from first setup to deeper product education without hitting a dead end.

The result was not dramatic overnight growth. What changed first was reader flow: more people moved from introductory pages to practical next-step content, and fewer sessions ended on isolated articles.
